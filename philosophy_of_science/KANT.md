# Kant: The Synthetic A Priori

## The Context

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) read Hume and, by his own account, was "awakened from dogmatic slumber." Hume had shown that causal necessity cannot be derived from observation. Kant agreed. But Kant thought Hume's conclusion — that causality is mere habit — was too deflationary. Science works. Newtonian mechanics makes precise, reliable predictions. Something must ground its necessity.

Kant's answer: the necessity does not come from the world. It comes from the mind. The mind does not passively receive experience — it actively structures it. Space, time, and causality are not features we observe in nature. They are the forms the mind imposes on raw sensory input in order to have experience at all.

## The Distinction

Kant drew a famous distinction:

- **Analytic** propositions: true by definition. "All bachelors are unmarried." The predicate is contained in the subject. These are necessarily true but uninformative — they tell you nothing new about the world.
- **Synthetic** propositions: the predicate adds something to the subject. "The cat is on the mat." These are informative but, on the standard empiricist view, only knowable through experience — contingent, not necessary.
- **A priori** knowledge: known independently of experience, through reason alone.
- **A posteriori** knowledge: known through experience.

Empiricists before Kant had assumed that all a priori knowledge is analytic (true by definition) and all synthetic knowledge is a posteriori (known through experience). Kant argued for a third category: **synthetic a priori** — propositions that are both informative and necessarily true, known prior to experience.

His examples: "Every event has a cause." "7 + 5 = 12." "The shortest path between two points is a straight line." These are not true by definition — they tell you something substantive. But they are not learned from experience either — they hold necessarily, for any possible experience.

## How This Saves Science

For Kant, the synthetic a priori propositions are the preconditions of experience. You cannot have experience at all without perceiving it as structured in space and time, and without interpreting events as causally connected. These are not claims about the world as it is in itself — they are claims about the structure the mind imposes on experience.

This saves scientific necessity: Newtonian mechanics is necessarily true — not because the world is necessarily that way, but because any experience we could have will be structured by Euclidean space, Newtonian time, and causal connection. The necessity is transcendental, not metaphysical.

## What It Costs

The cost is high. Kant's move confines science to the **phenomenal** realm — the world as experienced, structured by the mind's categories. The **noumenal** realm — the world as it is in itself, independent of any observer — is forever inaccessible.

This creates problems:

- When Riemann developed non-Euclidean geometry and Einstein used it for general relativity, Kant's claim that Euclidean geometry is a precondition of experience was falsified. The mind's categories are not fixed — they can be revised.
- The distinction between the world as experienced and the world as it is suggests a permanent gap between science and reality. Scientific realists find this unacceptable.
- The account of the synthetic a priori has never been fully worked out. What exactly makes something a precondition of experience, and how do we know when we have found one?

## The Legacy

Kant's influence on philosophy of science is enormous, mostly indirect. The idea that scientific frameworks impose structure on experience — rather than passively reflecting it — runs through Kuhn's paradigms, Lakatos's hard cores, and contemporary scientific pluralism. The idea that some commitments are preconditions of inquiry rather than empirical claims reappears in every account of theoretical frameworks and background assumptions.

But the specific doctrine of the synthetic a priori has not survived. Non-Euclidean geometry, relativistic spacetime, and quantum mechanics have all overturned claims Kant thought were preconditions of experience. The lesson is not that Kant was right about which propositions are synthetic a priori — he was wrong about most of them. The lesson is that every scientific framework has background commitments that function as if they were a priori, but which can in principle be revised. That is a Kantian insight, shorn of Kant's specific claims.
