# Popper: Falsificationism

## The Problem Popper Solved

Karl Popper (1902–1994) was a young man in Vienna when logical positivism was at its peak. He was dissatisfied with it, but his dissatisfaction crystallized around a different problem: the demarcation problem.

How do you distinguish science from non-science? The positivists used verifiability. Popper thought this was wrong for a different reason than the self-refutation argument: he noticed that the most impressive-looking theories — Freudian psychoanalysis, Adlerian psychology, Marxist history — seemed to be confirmed by everything. Every outcome was consistent with the theory. Every event could be reinterpreted to fit.

Meanwhile, Einstein's general relativity made a precise, risky prediction: starlight passing near the sun would be deflected by a specific amount. Eddington's 1919 eclipse expedition tested this. Had the deflection been different, relativity was dead. That is what made it science.

## The Asymmetry of Confirmation and Falsification

Popper's key insight: confirmation is logically weak, falsification is logically strong.

No number of confirming instances proves a universal law — this is Hume's point. But a single disconfirming instance refutes it. The inference from "this swan is black" to "not all swans are white" is deductively valid.

This asymmetry suggests replacing the search for confirmation with the search for falsification. Science does not accumulate evidence for theories — it eliminates theories that fail tests. What remains after many failed attempts at falsification is a theory that has survived — corroborated, not confirmed.

## Falsifiability as the Demarcation Criterion

Popper's proposal: a theory is scientific if and only if it is falsifiable — if there are possible observations that would, if they occurred, refute it.

Psychoanalysis fails this test. Whatever the patient does can be interpreted within the Freudian framework. There is no possible behavior that would count as evidence against it. For that reason, it is not science — not because it is false, but because it makes no testable predictions.

Marxist history fails similarly: no economic outcome could refute it, because the theory was flexible enough to accommodate any outcome.

Newton's mechanics passes: it makes precise predictions, and deviations from those predictions would refute it.

## Conjectures and Refutations

Popper's picture of scientific progress: bold conjectures, then attempts to refute them. Science advances not by accumulating truths but by eliminating errors. The method is:

1. Propose a hypothesis — the bolder the better, since bolder hypotheses are more falsifiable.
2. Deduce testable predictions.
3. Test rigorously, trying hard to refute.
4. If the test fails (hypothesis survives), it is corroborated — not confirmed.
5. If the test succeeds (hypothesis is refuted), discard and propose a new hypothesis.

The rationality of science lies entirely in step 3: the willingness to test honestly and accept refutation.

## What Popper Got Right

Popper correctly identified that the asymmetry between confirmation and falsification is real and important. He correctly saw that a theory that cannot be tested is scientifically empty. He correctly emphasized the role of risky predictions and genuine empirical risk. His account of the growth of scientific knowledge — not accumulation but error-elimination — captures something true about how science progresses.

## What Popper Got Wrong

**The Duhem-Quine problem.** When an experiment contradicts a prediction, it does not falsify the hypothesis alone — it falsifies the conjunction of the hypothesis with all the auxiliary assumptions used in the test (instrument calibration, background conditions, mathematical derivations). The scientist can always save the hypothesis by revising an auxiliary assumption. Falsification is never clean.

Popper was aware of this and had responses, but they were not fully satisfying. In practice, scientists do not discard theories at the first anomaly. They live with anomalies for years, investigating whether the problem is with the hypothesis or with the experimental setup.

**What to believe.** Falsificationism tells you what to discard but not what to believe. After a theory is corroborated by many tests, Popper says you should not conclude it is probably true — induction is still illegitimate. But scientists and engineers build bridges with Newtonian mechanics. They clearly believe it is approximately true for practical purposes. Popper's account cannot explain this.

**Ad hoc modifications.** Popper wanted to forbid "immunizing" a theory against falsification by adding ad hoc modifications. But he could not give a precise account of what makes a modification ad hoc versus legitimate. Lakatos built his entire programme around solving this problem.

## The Legacy

Popper transformed the philosophy of science. The idea that science is characterized by testability — that unfalsifiable theories are empty — is now part of the standard scientific worldview. His emphasis on critical testing over confirmation-seeking influenced experimental design and statistical practice. But his account is incomplete, and the completion is provided, in different ways, by Kuhn, Lakatos, and the Bayesian tradition.
