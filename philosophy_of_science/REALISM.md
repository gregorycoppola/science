# Scientific Realism: Do Theories Describe Reality?

## The Question

Scientific realism is the view that successful scientific theories are approximately true descriptions of reality — including the unobservable parts. Electrons, quarks, spacetime curvature, genes — these are real things, not just useful fictions. When a theory works, it is because it is getting something right about the way the world actually is.

Antirealism is the denial of this. The antirealist says: theories need only be empirically adequate — they need only correctly predict observable phenomena. Whether they accurately describe the unobservable furniture of the universe is a further question we cannot answer, and perhaps need not.

This is one of the oldest and still most live debates in philosophy of science.

## The No-Miracles Argument

The strongest argument for realism is the no-miracles argument, associated with Hilary Putnam (1975):

The predictive success of science would be miraculous if our theories were not at least approximately true. How else could Newtonian mechanics predict planetary orbits to many decimal places? How else could quantum electrodynamics predict the electron's magnetic moment to ten significant figures? If the theories were not latching onto real structure in the world, their success would be an extraordinary coincidence.

The best explanation of scientific success is that the theories are approximately true. Inference to the best explanation licenses realism.

## The Pessimistic Meta-Induction

The strongest argument against realism is the pessimistic meta-induction, associated with Larry Laudan (1981):

The history of science is a graveyard of successful theories that were later discovered to be false — not just approximately wrong, but fundamentally mistaken about the furniture of the world. Phlogiston, caloric fluid, the luminiferous ether, Newtonian absolute space, the steady-state universe. All were successful in their day; all were abandoned.

By induction on the history of science: our current theories, however successful, will likely be abandoned as fundamentally mistaken. We have no reason to believe they are approximately true. The no-miracles argument proves too much — it would have licensed realism about phlogiston in 1780.

## The Debate

Neither argument is conclusive, and the debate has continued for decades. Some responses:

**Selective realism** (Psillos, Kitcher): we should be realists only about those parts of a theory that are doing the predictive work — the "working posits." The ether was not a working posit; the electromagnetic field equations were. We should be realists about the latter, not the former. The pessimistic meta-induction applies to the scaffolding, not the structure.

**Structural realism** (Worrall, Ladyman): what science preserves across theory change is not the ontology (what things exist) but the structure (the mathematical relations between them). Maxwell's equations survived the transition from ether to field theory; the structure was retained even as the interpretation changed. We should be realists about structure, not about unobservable objects.

**Constructive empiricism** (van Fraassen): the goal of science is empirical adequacy — correct predictions about observable phenomena — not truth about unobservables. We should believe our best theories are empirically adequate, and remain agnostic about unobservables. The no-miracles argument fails because the criterion for theory acceptance is empirical adequacy, and an empirically adequate theory can achieve its goal without being true.

## The Bayesian Angle

The realism debate maps onto the Bayesian framework in a specific way. A realist says: the posterior over theories should be interpreted as a probability over claims about the unobservable world. An antirealist says: the posterior is just a device for organizing predictions about observables.

The Kolmogorov framework takes a realist-friendly position without fully settling the debate. The Solomonoff prior assigns higher probability to simpler theories — theories that compress the data more. If the world has deep structure (and the success of science suggests it does), then the simplest theories will be the ones that have latched onto that structure. The compression achieved by our best physical theories is not a coincidence — it reflects real regularities. This supports a moderate realism: the structure our theories identify is real, even if the ontological interpretation of the theoretical terms is uncertain.

The no-miracles argument, reformulated in Kolmogorov terms: if our theories were not tracking real structure, they would not achieve such high compression ratios. The compression is the evidence for realism.

## Where the Debate Stands

Scientific realism remains contested. The no-miracles argument and the pessimistic meta-induction are both compelling and neither is decisive. The most defensible position is probably a form of structural realism: the mathematical structures our best theories identify are real and are preserved (approximately) across theory change, even as the ontological interpretation of the theoretical vocabulary changes. This is consistent with the Kolmogorov framework and consistent with the history of science.

The question of whether there is more to scientific truth than structural accuracy — whether electrons are "really real" in some further sense — may be a question that the methods of science cannot settle, and perhaps one that does not need to be settled for science to proceed.
