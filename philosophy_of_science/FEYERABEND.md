# Feyerabend: Against Method

## The Provocateur

Paul Feyerabend (1924–1994) was Popper's student, Lakatos's friend, and philosophy of science's most gleeful iconoclast. His book *Against Method* (1975) argued that there is no single scientific method, that every proposed methodological rule has been violated by the scientists we most admire, and that the only rule consistent with the history of science is: anything goes.

This is not a position most scientists find comfortable. It was not meant to be.

## The Historical Argument

Feyerabend's main move was historical. He took the most celebrated episode in the history of science — the Copernican revolution and Galileo's role in it — and argued that Galileo succeeded not by following methodological rules but by violating them.

The Ptolemaic system had more empirical support than the Copernican system at the time Galileo championed it. The Copernican system contradicted common sense (if the Earth moves, why don't we feel it?), contradicted the physics of the day (objects fall straight down, not in curves), and made predictions — stellar parallax — that could not be confirmed with the instruments available.

Galileo's response was not to address these objections by normal scientific means. He used rhetoric, propaganda, and the newly invented telescope (whose reliability was contested) to win converts. He appealed to an alternative physics (inertia) that did not yet exist. He ignored or dismissed legitimate objections. He was, by the methodological standards of his day, behaving badly.

And he was right to do so. The Copernican system was correct. The methodological rules of 1615 would have killed it in the cradle.

Feyerabend's conclusion: methodological rules are historically contingent, context-dependent, and routinely violated in the most productive episodes of science. There is no universal method.

## What Feyerabend Was Really Arguing

Feyerabend's "anything goes" is often misread as nihilism — the view that science has no epistemic authority, that one theory is as good as another. That is not quite what he argued.

His deeper point: science is a human activity, embedded in history and culture. Its standards evolve. What counts as good evidence, legitimate inference, and acceptable method changes over time. Any attempt to freeze those standards into a universal methodology will be wrong — it will either exclude productive science (as naive falsificationism would have excluded Galileo) or be so loose as to be vacuous.

Feyerabend also argued against the privileged status of science relative to other ways of knowing — traditional medicine, indigenous knowledge, religious cosmologies. Not because these are epistemically equivalent to science, but because the boundary between science and non-science is more porous and more politically charged than scientists typically acknowledge.

## What He Got Right

Feyerabend was right that no simple methodological rule correctly classifies all and only good science. The history of science is messier than any philosopher's reconstruction. Scientists use rhetoric, exploit accidents, pursue hunches, ignore objections, and sometimes succeed precisely because they refused to follow the rules.

He was right that methodological standards are not eternal — they have changed and will change again. He was right that the social and political dimensions of science are real and cannot be cleanly separated from its epistemology.

## What He Got Wrong

The historical argument proves less than Feyerabend claimed. The fact that Galileo violated the methodological standards of his day does not show that all methodological standards are arbitrary. It shows that the standards of 1615 were wrong. Galileo was right, and his rightness was not just lucky — it was based on real evidence (the telescope observations), real arguments (the new mechanics), and real compression of the data. The Bayesian/Kolmogorov framework validates Galileo's choice retrospectively: the Copernican programme achieved lower total description length than the Ptolemaic programme.

"Anything goes" as a normative principle is false. Some theories really are better than others, in ways that can be made precise. The difficulty of specifying a universal method does not entail that method is absent — it entails that the method is more complex than any simple slogan captures.

## The Legacy

Feyerabend is more useful as a critic than as a constructive theorist. His historical arguments keep philosophers honest about the gap between rational reconstructions and the messy reality of science. His insistence on the political and social dimensions of science influenced science studies and the sociology of scientific knowledge. His provocations remain valuable, even if his conclusions overreach.
