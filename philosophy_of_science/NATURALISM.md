# Naturalism: Philosophy Continuous With Science

## The Position

Naturalism in the philosophy of science is the view that philosophy of science is not prior to science — it is continuous with it. There is no standpoint outside of science from which to evaluate science's methods, foundations, or claims to knowledge. The philosopher of science is doing science, not something above or below it.

The two main figures are W. V. O. Quine (1908–2000) and Larry Laudan (1941–).

## Quine's Epistemological Naturalism

Quine's "Epistemology Naturalized" (1969) argued that the traditional project of epistemology — providing a philosophical foundation for knowledge that is prior to and more certain than science — had failed, and should be abandoned.

The traditional project, from Descartes through Carnap, sought a foundation: basic certainties (clear and distinct ideas, sense data, protocol sentences) from which all knowledge could be derived or justified. Every attempt had failed. The foundation was never secure enough to bear the weight placed on it.

Quine's proposal: instead of looking for a foundation outside science, do epistemology as a chapter of science — specifically, as a chapter of psychology and cognitive science. How do humans actually form beliefs? What processes lead from sensory stimulation to scientific theories? These are empirical questions, answerable by empirical methods.

This is a radical move. It means that epistemology cannot evaluate science from outside — it can only describe and refine from within. There is no view from nowhere. The criteria for good reasoning are themselves subject to revision in light of experience.

## The Web of Belief

Quine's holism, developed in "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" (1951), is the philosophical basis for naturalism. Quine argued that our beliefs form a web, facing experience as a corporate body. No single belief faces experience directly — when an experiment contradicts predictions, we can revise any of the beliefs implicated in generating those predictions.

Some beliefs are near the periphery of the web and are revised readily (observational claims, specific empirical hypotheses). Others are near the center and are revised only under extreme pressure (logic, mathematics, fundamental physics). But no belief is in principle immune from revision. The analytic/synthetic distinction — the positivist claim that some truths are immune from empirical revision — is a dogma, not a principle.

This holism supports naturalism: if no statement faces experience alone, there is no foundational layer that epistemology could certify prior to scientific inquiry. The web is revised as a whole, by the standards it has developed through its own history.

## Laudan's Normative Naturalism

Larry Laudan accepted Quine's rejection of foundationalism but resisted the purely descriptive naturalism it seemed to imply. If we can only describe how scientists reason, we lose the ability to say how they should reason. Philosophy of science would collapse into sociology.

Laudan's response: the norms of scientific method are themselves empirical hypotheses, to be evaluated by whether they achieve their goals. The goal of science is empirical progress — theories that are more accurate, more powerful, more unified than their predecessors. Methodological norms are means to this end, and like all means, they can be evaluated by whether they work.

This is normative naturalism: the norms of science are not discovered by a priori philosophical analysis, but neither are they merely described. They are evaluated by their track record in achieving scientific goals — an empirical evaluation.

## The Connection to Bayesian Naturalism

The Bayesian/Kolmogorov framework is naturalist in Quine's sense: it grounds the norms of inference in the structure of computation, not in a priori philosophical analysis. The Solomonoff prior is derived, not stipulated. The update rule — Bayes' rule — is derived from the Dutch book argument (a coherence condition) and Cox's theorem (consistency with logic).

The norms of scientific method — prefer simpler theories, update on evidence, require testability — follow from the framework rather than being imposed on it. This is the naturalist ideal: norms that emerge from a principled account of what rational inference consists in, rather than being decreed by philosophical fiat.

What Quine sought — an epistemology that is continuous with science — is realized when the framework for evaluating scientific inference is itself a scientific theory: a theory of optimal prediction in computable environments.
