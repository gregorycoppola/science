# Kuhn: Paradigms and Scientific Revolutions

## The Challenge to Popper

Thomas Kuhn (1922–1996) was a physicist turned historian of science. His *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) is probably the most widely read book in the philosophy of science. Its central claim: Popper's account of science is a rational reconstruction that does not match how science actually works.

Scientists do not spend their time trying to falsify their own theories. They spend it solving puzzles within an accepted framework. And when anomalies accumulate — when experiments seem to contradict the theory — scientists typically do not discard the theory. They look for errors in the experimental setup, modify auxiliary assumptions, and continue. The theory is protected.

## Normal Science and Paradigms

Kuhn introduced the concept of a **paradigm**: a framework of assumptions, methods, standards, and exemplary problem-solutions that defines what counts as a legitimate scientific problem and what counts as an acceptable solution.

Under a paradigm, science proceeds as **normal science**: puzzle-solving within the accepted framework. Scientists are not questioning the framework — they are extending it, applying it to new problems, increasing its precision. This is the overwhelming majority of scientific activity.

Normal science is not uncritical. It is highly disciplined and rigorous. But its rigor is directed toward fitting new cases into the existing framework, not toward questioning the framework itself.

Anomalies — results that the paradigm cannot explain — are a normal feature of science. They accumulate slowly and are typically set aside: perhaps the instruments are wrong, perhaps the effect is real but peripheral, perhaps a clever modification of the theory will account for it. The paradigm is not abandoned at the first anomaly.

## Scientific Revolutions

A **scientific revolution** occurs when anomalies accumulate to the point where confidence in the paradigm is shaken, and a new paradigm is proposed that handles both the old successes and the new anomalies.

The revolution is not a smooth transition. The new paradigm is not simply the old paradigm plus a patch. It redefines the basic concepts, redraws the boundaries of the field, and changes what counts as a good explanation. Kuhn called this **incommensurability**: the two paradigms are not strictly comparable, because they define their terms differently.

Examples: the Copernican revolution did not simply add heliocentrism to Ptolemaic astronomy — it changed what "planet" meant, what counted as a celestial body, what kind of mathematics was appropriate. The Lavoisier revolution did not add oxygen to phlogiston theory — it eliminated phlogiston and reconceived combustion, acids, and the atmosphere.

## The Sociological Element

Kuhn emphasized that paradigm shifts are not purely rational events. The old guard typically does not convert — it dies out. Young scientists, less invested in the old framework, adopt the new one. Social factors — prestige, institutions, funding — influence which anomalies are taken seriously and which are set aside.

This was taken by many readers — especially in the social sciences and humanities — as meaning that science is not rational, that paradigm choice is purely sociological, that there is no objective basis for preferring one paradigm over another.

Kuhn himself resisted this reading. He thought there were rational criteria for paradigm choice — accuracy, scope, simplicity, fruitfulness, consistency. But he acknowledged these criteria underdetermine the choice and can conflict with each other.

## What Kuhn Got Right

Kuhn was right that science operates within frameworks that are not themselves tested in the normal course of research. He was right that anomalies are tolerated, not immediately acted upon. He was right that scientific revolutions involve conceptual restructuring, not just addition of new facts. He was right that the sociology of science matters and cannot be cleanly separated from its epistemology.

## What Kuhn Got Wrong (Or Left Incomplete)

Incommensurability is overstated. Scientists communicating across paradigms manage to compare them — imperfectly, with effort, but not impossibly. The pessimistic reading of incommensurability, that there is no rational basis for paradigm choice, does not follow from Kuhn's own examples.

Kuhn's account of revolutions lacks a normative dimension. He describes how paradigm shifts happen but does not say when they should happen. Under the Bayesian/Kolmogorov framework, a paradigm shift is rational when the new paradigm achieves lower total description length — when the accumulated anomalies (high `L(D | H)` under the old paradigm) plus the complexity of the old paradigm (high `L(H)`) exceeds the cost of the new one. This gives a precise answer to the question Kuhn left open.

## The Legacy

*The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* changed how people think about science. The word "paradigm" entered the general vocabulary. The idea that science has a sociology as well as a logic is now uncontroversial. But Kuhn's account called out for a more precise treatment of the rational structure of paradigm choice — and Lakatos, and later the Bayesian tradition, provided it.
