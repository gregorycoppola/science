# Hume: The Problem of Induction

## The Problem

David Hume (1711–1776) identified the deepest problem in the philosophy of science, and no one has fully solved it since.

The problem of induction: there is no logical justification for inferring a general law from a finite set of observations.

More precisely: the inference from "all observed Fs are Gs" to "all Fs are Gs" is not deductively valid. The conclusion could be false even if every premise is true. No number of confirming instances makes the inference logically necessary.

You can observe one million white swans. The one-million-and-first might be black. It was, in Australia.

## Why This Is Devastating

The problem is not just that induction might fail occasionally. It is that induction has no rational justification at all, if justification means logical entailment.

The obvious response: induction is justified because it has worked in the past. We used inductive reasoning and it led to reliable science, reliable technology, reliable predictions. Therefore induction is reliable.

Hume's reply: this argument is itself an inductive argument. You are using induction to justify induction. The justification is circular. A method cannot validate itself.

This is not a defect in any particular inductive argument — it is a structural feature of induction as such. No non-circular justification of induction exists.

## What Hume Concluded

Hume's own conclusion was that inductive inference is a matter of habit and custom, not reason. We expect the future to resemble the past not because we have a rational argument for it, but because our minds are built to form such expectations. Causation — the cornerstone of scientific reasoning — is not something we observe in the world. We observe constant conjunction: A followed by B, repeatedly. The "necessary connection" between cause and effect is a projection of the mind, not a feature of reality.

This is a deeply unsettling conclusion. It means that science, at its foundations, rests on psychological habit rather than rational justification.

## Responses to Hume

Every major philosophy of science after Hume is, in part, a response to this problem.

**Kant** tried to save necessary causal connections by arguing they are preconditions of experience — the mind imposes them, so they are guaranteed to hold for any possible experience. This saves causality but at the cost of confining it to the phenomenal realm.

**Popper** dissolved the problem by arguing that science does not rely on induction at all. Science proceeds by deduction: bold conjectures, then attempts to falsify them. Confirmation is not the goal — refutation is.

**Bayesians** reframe the problem. The question is not "can we be certain?" but "what should we believe, given the evidence?" Induction becomes not a logical entailment but a probability update. Hume's problem shows you cannot get certainty; it does not show you cannot get rational degrees of belief.

**Solomonoff** grounds the Bayesian update in computation. The prior is not arbitrary — it is the universal prior over computable hypotheses. Induction becomes: find the shortest program consistent with the observations. This is not a solution to Hume in the logical sense — it does not provide deductive certainty. But it provides a principled, optimal strategy for prediction, which is all science needs.

## The Permanent Residue

Hume's problem cannot be dissolved. There is no deductive justification for induction, and there never will be. Any response that claims to fully solve the problem is overstating its case.

What can be done — and what the Bayesian/Kolmogorov framework does — is show that the lack of deductive certainty does not prevent rational inference. You do not need certainty to reason well. You need a coherent prior, a likelihood function, and a commitment to update by Bayes' rule. The optimal prior is the Solomonoff prior. The resulting inference is provably the best possible strategy for prediction in any computable environment.

This is the most that can be asked for. Hume showed we cannot have more.
