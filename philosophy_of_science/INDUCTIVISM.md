# Inductivism: The Naive View

## The Position

Inductivism is the view that science proceeds by:

1. Making many observations, without prejudice.
2. Identifying patterns and regularities in those observations.
3. Inducing general laws from the patterns.
4. Deriving predictions from the laws and testing them.

Francis Bacon (1561–1626) is the founding figure. In the *Novum Organum* (1620), he argued that the scholastic method — deducing conclusions from Aristotelian first principles — was sterile. The alternative was systematic empirical observation: collect facts, build up from them, resist the temptation to theorize prematurely.

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) gave inductivism its most systematic formulation. Mill's methods — agreement, difference, concomitant variation, residues — are algorithms for identifying causal relationships from observations. If two situations share only one antecedent condition and both produce the same effect, that condition is likely the cause.

## What Is Right About It

Inductivism captures something real about scientific practice. Science is empirical — it bottoms out in observation. Theories that contradict observations must give way. The discipline of attending carefully to data, resisting armchair theorizing, and building up from evidence is genuinely important.

Mill's methods, in modern form, are the basis of experimental design, epidemiology, and causal inference from observational data. They are not wrong — they are incomplete.

## What Is Wrong With It

The logical problem is fatal. No finite set of observations entails a universal law. From "every swan I have observed is white," you cannot deduce "all swans are white." The deduction fails for any finite sample. This is Hume's problem, addressed in `HUME.md`.

Beyond the logical problem, inductivism misrepresents how science actually works. Scientists do not observe without prejudice — they observe with specific hypotheses in mind, using instruments designed to test those hypotheses, within a theoretical framework that determines what counts as a relevant observation. "Pure" observation, free of theoretical contamination, does not exist. The instruction to collect facts "without bias" is not even coherent — you need a framework to decide which facts to collect.

Einstein did not induce special relativity from observations of moving trains. Darwin did not induce evolution from observations of finches. The great theoretical advances in science have come from bold imaginative leaps — hypotheses generated by insight, then tested against observations. Inductivism has the order wrong.

## The Legacy

Inductivism remains the folk epistemology of science — the view that most working scientists would articulate if asked how science works. It captures the importance of empirical grounding. But as a philosophical account of scientific inference, it collapsed under Hume's criticism and has not recovered. Every subsequent philosophy of science is an attempt to do better.
